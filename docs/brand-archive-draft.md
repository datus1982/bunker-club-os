## 2. History Narrative

### Pre-opening

Hailey and Ian McDermid opened The Pump Bar at 2425 N. Walker Ave. in the Uptown 23rd District in December 2014, converting a former Texaco service station (originally owned by "Chili Mac," a pro sports player, in the late 1920s–early 1930s, with rumored bootlegging operations). The Pump became a "massive hit" (OKC Gazette) and established the McDermids as one of OKC's leading bar operators.

On January 4, 2016, the Bunker Club was announced simultaneously on the local podcast *We Apologize for the Inconvenience* and on OKCTalk.com by forum administrator Pete, who had visited the space with Ian the prior week. Ian McDermid himself confirmed in post #11 of that thread: "Thanks for the write-up Pete! Thank you also for waiting on us before you published. We did the reveal on a podcast with our neighbors at We Apologize For The Inconvenience yesterday."

The McDermids had leased the far-west retail space inside the Tower Theater building — a historic "jewel box" storefront with restored rare green Vitrolite glass. The space had been closed for a long time before their acquisition. Ian described the theme as "post war atomic era dive bar" with design elements and sounds from the Cold War.

Construction was targeted for spring 2016 but faced significant delays: lease issues involving ADA compliance through a back service door, the need to completely redo the concrete floors, and the owners' decision to self-perform most of the work. A building permit was submitted in April 2016, construction began in earnest by May–July 2016, and an October/November 2016 opening was projected by September 2016.

By February 2017 the sign was up and interior photography showed the finished design. Ian posted on February 18, 2017: "We plan on opening in a couple of weeks. So many little details/features and most of the work is self performed."

### Opening

Bunker Club opened on **April 2, 2017**, as part of the Open Streets OKC event. OKCTalk's Pete posted that same morning: "From their FB Page... They open today at 11AM as part of Open Streets." He visited that day and reported: "Super, super cool place."

Early community reviews were immediately positive. OKCTalk users visiting within the first month noted: "Great vibe. Good beer selection. Friendly and enthusiastic bar tenders. Definitely gonna be a hit." (April 24, 2017, OKCTalk #84). A 2019 OKCTalk post described the JFK cocktail as "seriously my fav craft cocktail in OKC. dangerously smooth and refreshing."

A sidewalk patio with furniture and railings (plus planters in production) was added by mid-2019, noted in OKCTalk posts from July 2019.

### Ownership Change (2023)

On May 25, 2023, OKCTalk user BoulderSooner posted: "announced yesterday that Steven Tyler and a group are taking over this concept." Multiple review aggregators describe this as a June 2023 transition, with the new ownership "restoring the bar to its former glory" including a full inventory, new menu items, and expanded event programming (trivia, movie nights, karaoke, bingo, burlesque). Stephen Tyler had a prior connection to the Tower Theater complex — Ian McDermid mentioned "Stephen Tyler's new creative studio at the tower theater" in his January 2016 OKCTalk post about the Bunker Club announcement.

### Voice guide (distilled)

1. **Tone: warm, wry, unhurried.** Ian McDermid's own writing voice — casual, self-deprecating, honest about delays — is the reference model. Never urgent, never promotional in a hard-sell way.
2. **The theme is decor, not ideology.** The founders' explicit framing: not a political bar, not a museum. "An ode to" — evocative, affectionate, not didactic.
3. **Immersive escape, not nostalgia tourism.** The Disneyland comparison is deliberate: you forget the outside world. The bar celebrates the feeling of the atomic age, acknowledges its anachronisms openly.
4. **Unpretentious but elevated.** This phrase from the original site captures the cocktail philosophy and extends to the whole venue: serious craft, zero attitude.
5. **Community-embedded.** Local artists made the art. Local bars supplied the decor. Neighboring restaurants are encouraged by carry-in policy. The bar is part of a block, not a standalone destination.
6. **Short, declarative.** Taglines run 2–5 words. Body copy avoids jargon. Hot dogs are described precisely: "natural case wiener, all meat chili, cheese, sweet onions, mustard or ketchup optional, paper towels."
7. **Never says:** "craft" (used generically), "curated" (too magazine-speak), "speakeasy," "secret," "exclusive," or anything that suggests velvet-rope energy. The Bunker is anti-pretension.
8. **Drinks have Cold War proper-noun names.** Manhattan Project, Dr. Strangelove, Sputnik, Black List, 3 Mile Island Iced Tea, J.F.K., Mind Bender Bomb — specific, historically grounded, slightly dark-humored.

## 4. Visual Style Archive

### bunkerclubokc.com (original site — accessed via HTTP, SSL expired)

**Structure:** Blog format (WordPress or similar). Three posts from January–February 2021, all credited to "admin." No visible navigation beyond HOME and POSTS. Minimal custom styling observed via browser rendering.

**Described interior elements** (from blog post copy):
- Heavy glass entry doors (described as "original," building-era)
- Maroon walls running floor to ceiling
- Gold-upholstered bar seats and curvy booths
- Sparkly Naugahyde "bass boat vinyl" (OKCTalk, per Urbanized post #16)
- Small round booths; cocktail-table video games (planned as free-play, per OKCTalk #1)
- Black and white TVs throughout
- Retro-futuristic overall decor
- Warm, dim lighting

**Artwork / signage (from OKCTalk thread and blog):**
- Near door: painting by **Mind Bender Tattoo** artists depicting "several famous political activists and communists, including Woody Guthrie of Oklahoma"
- Back murals + hand-lettering: **Fraidy Cat Signs, Tanner Fraidy**
- Exterior sign: large-format, described as "badass" (Pete, OKCTalk #51, November 9, 2016); described as fitting the historic jewel-box storefront perfectly
- Logo: noted as appearing "bitchin'" in community reaction; one poster joked it read "Veekya Club" (suggesting a stylized Cyrillic-influenced or Cold War propaganda aesthetic)
- DJ booth: vinyl-focused

**Color impression:** Warm, amber-to-maroon palette. Gold upholstery. Black-and-white TVs. The aesthetic is described as "lush" not stark.

### bunkerokc.com (current Toast-hosted site)

**Structure:** Single-page layout with anchor-linked sections (no separate URLs except /home#Events). Clean, minimal. Navigation bar with logo. Untappd partnership badge in footer.

**Typography/display:** The tagline "ATOMIC AGE HIGH-DIVE" appears in all-caps, suggesting a bold, display-weight treatment. No font stack identifiable from text extraction alone.

**Palette:** Not extractable from text fetch; the Toast default hosting template is visually generic. The terminal-green OS aesthetic in the Bunker Club OS is NOT derived from the public site — that is the ops tool's own design language (see CLAUDE.md and the LCARS framework).

### Alignment with Bunker Club OS terminal aesthetic

The bar's interior (maroon, gold, warm amber, dim) and the OS terminal (phosphor green on black) are deliberately distinct. The OS's aesthetic is internally code-named "Shelter Authority / BUNKER UNIFIED OS / Civil Defense" — which echoes the bar's Cold War theme thematically but uses a different visual register (monochrome CRT vs. warm analog). The public website should probably split the difference: reference the bar's warm interior palette while acknowledging the atomic-age framing. The OS green should not bleed into the public site's visual language.

## 8. Open Questions for the Owner

1. **Exact founding date for "about" copy:** April 2, 2017 is confirmed as the public soft-open (Open Streets OKC), but was there a later "grand opening" event? The OKCTalk thread ends on page 4 without a formal grand opening announcement.

2. **The "443" vs. "433" address discrepancy:** The December 2016 OKC Gazette article and the OKC Gazette March 2017 article both cite 443 NW 23rd; all other sources (bunkerokc.com, Untappd, Google, Yelp, OKCTalk) cite 433. Is 443 a persistent press typo or was there ever a suite-number variant?

3. **bunkerclubokc.com ownership and archival:** The domain's SSL is expired and the site is only accessible over HTTP. The three blog posts (Jan–Feb 2021) contain the richest founder-voice content. Who controls this domain — the McDermids or Stephen/current ownership? It should be archived before the DNS cutover or before it lapses. Wayback Machine access should be manually verified.

4. **The Pump Bar cross-reference:** Hailey and Ian McDermid's prior bar (2425 N. Walker Ave.) built the community and staff pipeline for Bunker Club. Several people who worked at The Pump (including Glen Bliss) moved to or contributed to Bunker Club. Is The Pump Bar still operating under the McDermids, or did the 2023 transition encompass that venue too?

5. **"Hailey McDermid" pronoun:** The blog post "The Bunker Club's Uniqueness" uses "he" for Hailey ("when he launched the Bunker Club"). All other available evidence (family photos referenced in OKCTalk, the McDermid partnership framing) is consistent with Hailey being female. This appears to be a proofreading error in the blog post. Confirm before quoting the original site's copy in any public-facing material.

6. **X/Twitter follower count and bio:** Could not retrieve due to paywall; owner or staff should confirm whether the account is still active and what the current bio reads.

7. **TikTok account status:** Only confirmed through the TikTok discover/search page; could not retrieve profile details. User-generated TikToks referencing the bar exist (#bunkerclubokc) but the brand account's own post volume and bio are UNVERIFIED.

8. **Oklahoma Gazette "Best of OKC 2018":** The article URL returned a 403. What category/award did Bunker Club win or appear in? This may be useful for "as seen in" copy.

9. **The "We Apologize for the Inconvenience" podcast episode:** Ian mentioned this was where Bunker Club was first revealed (Jan 3–4, 2016). The episode included discussion of The Pump's first year and "an anecdote about how I accidentally served a woman 16 shots of Jager at a black label society concert." This is a rich archival source — does the owner have access to or knowledge of this podcast?

10. **Opening phone number change:** The March 2017 OKC Gazette lists (405) 226-5030; the current number is (405) 482-6095. When did this change, and is there a third number active anywhere?
