## 1. NW 23rd Street × Route 66: Alignment Facts

### The alignment, year by year

NW 23rd Street was the primary east-west Route 66 corridor through Oklahoma City from the highway's commissioning in 1926 through the deletion of the Business Route in 1979 — **53 consecutive years** on the Mother Road.

| Period | Alignment entering from the west |
|--------|----------------------------------|
| 1926–1930 | West on 39th St → south on Classen Blvd → **east on 23rd St** |
| 1930–1933 | West on 39th St → south on Western Ave → **east on 23rd St** |
| 1933–1953 | West on May Ave → **east on 23rd St** |
| 1953–1979 | Expressway bypass opened (1953); NW 23rd redesignated **US 66 Business Route** |
| March 5, 1979 | US 66 Business Route deleted in Oklahoma City |
| April 1, 1985 | US Route 66 decommissioned statewide in Oklahoma |

Sources: VisitOKC Route 66 Alignments page (https://www.visitokc.com/things-to-do/route-66/route-66-alignments/); Wikipedia, "U.S. Route 66 in Oklahoma" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Route_66_in_Oklahoma); OKC Historic Context Project Report PDF (https://www.okhistory.org/shpo/docs/okcrt66.pdf — too large to fetch, abstract confirmed by search summary).

### 433 NW 23rd Street (Bunker Club)

- **Directly on the historic alignment.** The Route 66 roadway ran along this exact address from at least 1926 to 1979.
- Adjacent address: Tower Theatre at 425 NW 23rd (same block). Bunker Club at 433 NW 23rd.
- National Geographic named Bunker Club by name in its 2026 Route 66 OKC guide: "Other throwback pleasures include the dive bar Bunker Club." Source: https://www.nationalgeographic.com/travel/article/paid-content-creative-guide-route-66-oklahoma-city
- Yelp describes Bunker Club as "an atomic age dive bar in OKC's Uptown 23rd District on historic Route 66." Source: https://www.yelp.com/biz/bunker-club-oklahoma-city-2
- TravelOK lists Bunker Club in its official Route 66 tourism directory. Source: https://www.travelok.com/listings/view.profile/id.26570

### What "Business Route" means for the story

When the 1953 expressway bypass was completed, through-traffic left NW 23rd. But the **Business Route** designation kept NW 23rd as the official "old highway" alignment for another 26 years, and the street continued as OKC's primary midtown commercial strip through the early 1980s. The shift from highway to business route is itself part of the story arc: NW 23rd went from national highway to local main street, which mirrors the Bunker Club's Cold War "aftermath" aesthetic.

## 2. Building, Block & District History Timeline

### 433 NW 23rd — what's known

No independent historic documentation for 433 NW 23rd specifically was located. The building is adjacent to the Tower Theatre block (425 NW 23rd). The 400-block of NW 23rd was part of the commercial corridor that developed in the 1920s–1950s.

The lot at 411 NW 23rd housed Ed Reynolds Flowers from at least 1946 through the 1990s (now 23rd Street Body Piercing), confirmed by OHS Meyers/Hillerman Collection photograph dated February 15, 1946 (https://gateway.okhistory.org/ark:/67531/metadc1121100/). Margolies also photographed this storefront in 1993 (OKC Mod, "Roadside Oklahoma Through the Eyes of John Margolies, Part 2": https://okcmod.com/2018/01/roadside-oklahoma-through-the-eyes-of-john-margolies-part-2/). The 400-block appears to have been established retail by the 1940s.

**OPEN QUESTION FOR OWNER:** What does the owner know about 433 NW 23rd's pre-bar history? What stood there before the current building? Oklahoma County Assessor records and Sanborn fire insurance maps (available via OKH or local library) would give the construction date and earlier uses.

### Tower Theatre (425 NW 23rd) — the anchor

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| March 1, 1937 | Building permit issued; cost $35,000 |
| July 15, 1937 | Tower Theatre opens. Architect: **W. Scott Dunne** (Dallas). Style: Art Deco/Streamline Moderne. Capacity: 1,200–1,500 seats. Pipe organ, working stage, scarlet waterfall curtains. |
| 1937–1963 | Operated by Standard Theaters Corporation. First-run films; the street is in its Route 66 heyday. Adjacent TG&Y dime store. |
| April 1965–November 1966 | *The Sound of Music* runs **82 consecutive weeks** — still the longest theatrical run in the building's history. |
| January 16, 1967 | Three-alarm fire guts interior; $200,000 in damage. Reopened March 22, 1967. |
| January 1985 | Regular movie showings end. |
| November 3, 2005 | Property transferred to Uptown Development Group, LLC. |
| 2014 | Pivot Project begins redevelopment. |
| April 2016 | Reopens as live music venue. |
| 2017 | Bunker Club opens at 433 NW 23rd (adjacent). |
| June 2023 | Bunker Club changes ownership; "restored to its former glory." |

Sources: Cinema Treasures (https://cinematreasures.org/theaters/11609); Retro Metro OKC (https://www.retrometrookc.org/tower-theatre/); Uptown 23rd History (https://uptown23rd.com/history); VisitOKC (https://www.visitokc.com/listing/tower-theatre/2089/).

### The NW 23rd commercial corridor

- **1922** — Oklahoma City University established at 23rd & N. Blackwelder; the district gains an anchor.
- **1926** — NW 23rd becomes Route 66; cross-country motorists drive the block.
- **1930** — Milk Bottle Grocery built at 24th & Classen (the Route 66 corner, one block from Bunker Club).
- **1937** — Tower Theatre opens; the block becomes an entertainment destination.
- **1948** — Giant milk bottle installed atop the Milk Bottle Grocery building; becomes an OKC icon.
- **1950s** — NW 23rd is described as "the first major shopping location away from downtown OKC" with angled street parking, packed retailers across four-plus blocks. Businesses documented on or near 23rd: O'Mealey's Cafeteria, Veazey's Drug, Toddle House, Patrick's Foods, and many others.
- **1958** — Gold Dome (1112 NW 23rd) opens, designed by Robert Roloff; one of the first geodesic domes used as a commercial building in the US. On the Route 66 alignment.
- **1967** — Tower Theatre fire and recovery: a dramatic chapter in the block's story.
- **1979** — US 66 Business Route deleted; Route 66 chapter officially closes.
- **Late 1980s** — Tower Theatre closes; the district enters decline.
- **2014–2016** — Tower Theatre redeveloped as entertainment anchor; Uptown 23rd revival begins.

Sources: Uptown 23rd History (https://uptown23rd.com/history); OKC Mod 23rd Street series (https://okcmod.com/2018/07/travelling-through-time-along-23rd-street-part-3/); 405 Magazine Tower Theater piece (https://www.405magazine.com/memories-of-the-tower-theater/).

### The Milk Bottle Grocery — Route 66 icon one block away

Built 1930 as a small corner grocery; the enormous milk bottle added 1948. Classen Boulevard was part of the original 1926–1930 Route 66 alignment through OKC, making the Milk Bottle directly on the earliest alignment. Now on the National Register of Historic Places. This is one block from 433 NW 23rd — a strong "then and now" narrative anchor.
